ClimateWins Weather Prediction

Using machine learning to predict extreme weather patterns for European climate preparedness

The Challenge

ClimateWins, a European nonprofit focused on climate preparedness, wanted a way to anticipate extreme weather events before they occur. With climate change increasing the frequency and severity of these events, the organization required predictive capabilities to enable proactive resource allocation and community preparation.

The Approach

I developed and refined machine learning models using historical weather data from across Europe, implementing both supervised and unsupervised learning techniques.

The modeling process involved extensive data cleaning and feature engineering to ensure accuracy. I tested multiple algorithms including decision trees, random forests, and neural networks to determine which provided the most reliable predictions for different weather phenomena.

Technical Hurdles & Solutions

Data Quality: Historical weather data contained gaps and inconsistencies across different European regions. Some data was removed and others "borrowed" from nearby regions to fill small gaps.

Model Accuracy vs. Interpretability: Complex neural networks provided high accuracy (especially after optimization), but stakeholders will need to follow too. I balanced this by creating feature importance visualizations to show which weather indicators most strongly predicted pleasant weather.

Communicating to Non-Technical Audiences: Climate scientists and emergency planners needed actionable insights, not just model outputs. I generated visualizations using various python options to show accuracy and create confidence via anticipating questions and storytelling through thought experiments (potentially adding nuance and further discussion).

The Results

I wasn't able to complete all the goals that ClimateWins was attempting to remedy myself with the data I had. However, I was able to prove that machine learning models could reliably predict pleasant weather based on past weather (again, this was binary; pleasant or not). Still, having such great success with a relatively simple problem could lay the groundwork for even more complex systems that include more robust data that would be useful in the future.

Project Information

  • Category: Machine Learning
  • Tools: Python, scikit-learn, pandas, NumPy, matplotlib, seaborn
  • Techniques: Supervised & Unsupervised Learning
  • Date: 2025
  • Context: CareerFoundry Project

Key Findings

Successfully trained models to identify patterns in historical weather data and predict pleasant weather events with strong accuracy across multiple cities within Europe.

Impact

The predictive models enable predictions of pleasant weather from 15 locations using temperature and precipitation as primary indicators.

Visualizations show the models finding those patterns and highlighting variable importances.
