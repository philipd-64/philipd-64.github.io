Video Games Sales Analysis (GameCo)

Analyzing 30+ years of regional sales data to identify market shifts and guide strategic planning

The Challenge

GameCo's executive board assumed video game sales per region had remained stable over time and needed insights into best and worst performing genres to stay competitive. With their 2017 marketing budget on the line, they needed data-driven answers about regional market dynamics and genre performance to properly allocate resources and demonstrate to investors their ability to grow even as competitors struggled.

The Approach

I analyzed 30+ years of video game sales data (1980-2016) using Excel, examining regional trends across North America, Europe, Japan, and other markets. The analysis focused on two key questions: whether regional sales had truly remained stable over time, and which genres were performing best and worst globally and by region.

I created line charts to visualize sales trends over time, calculating each region's percentage of global sales by year to identify market share shifts. For genre analysis, I sorted all genres by global sales to identify the top and bottom performers, then analyzed regional variations to understand market-specific preferences. I deliberately excluded incomplete 2016 year-to-date data from the analysis since it ended in October, before the critical Christmas sales season.

Technical Hurdles & Solutions

Identifying Real Trends vs. Market Volatility: The 1980-2000 period showed extreme volatility in a sort of "gold rush" phase where North America and Japan frequently traded market leadership positions. I used percentage of global sales rather than absolute numbers to normalize this volatility and identify when genuine stable trends emerged (around 2000-2010) versus when new lasting shifts began (post-2010).

Choosing the Right Visualizations: While histograms or bar charts could have worked, I selected line charts because trends are, arguably, easier to read (especially individual slopes). For the final presentation to executives, I simplified complex data by filtering to show only the top 3 and bottom 3 genres separately for clarity, making the insights actionable rather than overwhelming.

Missing Context About Mobile Gaming: The data revealed a significant market shift starting around 2010 which I hypothesied was because of iPhone's release in 2007 and subsequent mobile gaming adoption. However, the dataset didn't track mobile game sales, potentially skewing the traditional console/PC sales data. I flagged this hypothesis for stakeholders as mobile gaming's rise might explain declining traditional sales without indicating actual market contraction.

The Results

The analysis definitively disproved the executive board's assumption that regional sales had remained stable. After achieving relative stability from 2000-2010, a market shift occurred around 2010: North American market share began a consistent decline while European sales continued steady growth, fundamentally changing the global gaming landscape.

Genre analysis revealed Action, Shooter, and Sports as top global performers, while Adventure, Puzzle, and Strategy were least enticing to the overall market. Importantly, Japan showed distinctly different preferences. Role-Playing games dominated until 2015 when Action games finally overtook them, highlighting the need for region-specific marketing strategies rather than one-size-fits-all approaches.

I recommended investigating what enabled the 2000-2010 stability period and what changed post-2010 to create new trends. Without staffing data in the dataset, I couldn't draw any solid conclusions, but I noted that understanding whether European growth resulted from increased staff investment or from a few high-performing individuals could be helpful for resource allocation decisions going forward.

Project Information

  • Category: Sales Analysis
  • Tools: Excel (Charts, Pivot Tables, Data Filtering)
  • Techniques: Trend Analysis, Market Segmentation, Percentage Calculations
  • Date: 2025
  • Context: CareerFoundry Project

Key Findings

Identified 2010 as pivotal inflection point where North American dominance ended and European market share began sustained growth—directly contradicting executive assumptions about stable regional performance.

Japan's distinct genre preferences (Role-Playing vs. global Action/Shooter preference) demonstrated need for region-specific strategies rather than uniform global approach.

Mobile gaming hypothesis: iPhone launch (2007) and subsequent mobile adoption may explain traditional gaming sales decline without indicating actual market contraction.

Impact

Analysis challenged fundamental business assumptions, providing data-driven evidence that European markets now warranted increased marketing investment despite historical North American focus.

Genre performance insights enabled targeted resource allocation—doubling down on Action/Shooter/Sports globally while tailoring Japan strategy around Role-Playing games' traditional strength.
